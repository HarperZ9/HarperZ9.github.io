And I do not think we have to wait for the current system to grow a conscience.

A creative economy can be built beside it.

Not another marketplace with softer branding. A community-owned infrastructure where the chain exists to support the structure instead of siphoning nutrients out of it.

The source graph preserves lineage. The licensing layer preserves consent. The rendering tools help make the work. The epistemic engine keeps contribution, uncertainty, and dispute visible. The treasury routes value back through the artists, maintainers, moderators, teachers, compute providers, and people doing the care work that keeps a community alive.

Each layer feeds the next without becoming its landlord.

That is accountable-from-the-bottom-up infrastructure. The people affected by a rule can inspect it, argue with it, change it, leave it, and carry their work somewhere else.

It will not be pure. We are still human. We will compete, hoard, form cliques, protect our friends, punish our enemies, and call whatever benefits us ethical.

Good. Design for the person who actually exists.

Keep the receipt. Who supplied the source? Who agreed? Who worked? Who maintained the tool? Who received the money? What remains disputed? Who changed the rule, and who benefited?

The goal is not a universal score for human worth. It is a surface we can reopen when power and memory begin editing the story.

This could create domains that barely have names yet: source-bounded generative art, cooperative rendering, community-owned creative models, open research studios, shared fabrication, local learning networks, service platforms that return time instead of consuming it.

Maybe the old system gets dismantled by building enough exits that its worst assumptions stop being necessary for survival.

And if we believe any of the ethics we keep performing in public, then we have to start by being honest about who we are.

All of us.

Capable of building the table. Capable of hoarding everything on it.

Build for that person, and give the community a way to see when the steward starts becoming a thief.
