A model can help somebody cross the gap between intuition and prototype. It can translate a paper. It can explain the same idea until one version finally sticks. It can help a person outside the academy discover that the question they have been circling already has a name. It can let one person attempt work that used to require a department and a budget.

I am one of the people who feels that opening in my bones. I missed the official road into research. I spent years treating that as a verdict on whether I was allowed to discover anything. These tools gave me a route back to the questions, or at least a route toward them, and I am not going to lie about what that means to me.

I am also not going to pretend the opening arrived clean.

Expert-shaped output is cheap now. Expertise is not. Generation got faster while verification stayed expensive, so the cost moved. Maintainers read the pull requests. Teachers figure out whether the student understands the code. Editors sort the plausible from the real. Researchers reconstruct the proof. Clients and audiences discover which polished surface had nothing under it.

I call that review debt. Somebody pays it, even when the invoice is hidden.

Creative workers pay another version. Their craft was never only a badge. It paid rent. It formed identity and community. It gave shape to years of practice. When a client can ask for ten plausible images in an afternoon, the illustrator does not receive a philosophical lesson about abstraction. They receive a lower rate or no call at all.

I have no patience for the people who answer that pain with, "Adapt." That is capitalist sadism in a futurist T-shirt.

The companies building foundation models are full of shit when they swallow enormous portions of human culture, convert it into private capability, meter access to the result, and call the rental democratization. A library does not become democratic because one company scanned it, sealed the scanner, and charges by the paragraph.

The evangelists are full of shit when they say "just a tool" as though tools have no owner, training history, labor effect, energy cost, supply chain, or politics. They celebrate the death of pedigree while paying monthly rent to a new landlord of cognition. They generate faster than they can understand and make somebody else clean the mess.

The purists can be full of shit too. They are right about extraction, imitated identities, collapsing rates, flooded markets, and the way corporations make dispossession sound inevitable. Some still turn suffering into a credential. Hours become moral worth. Scarcity becomes authenticity. Every hybrid practice becomes fraud, while the assistants, software, stock libraries, fabricators, samplers, unpaid interns, exploited vendors, and inherited conventions under "human-made" work disappear from view.

Human-made does not guarantee humane.

Institutions spent decades saying merit lived behind the correct tuition payment, internship, citation network, vocabulary, and manner of standing in the room. Now that AI has made the output surface porous, some of those same institutions have discovered a sacred concern for process.

Capital has its own little joke. It promises that productivity will free us, then turns every saved hour into fewer workers, more surveillance, or a larger margin. It says abundance is coming and makes sure the means remain scarce.

And I am using an AI system to help write this.

There. No reveal. No gotcha.

I gave it my old essays, public comments, research notes, personal history, arguments, revisions, and a lot of angry feedback when it produced prose that sounded like a tasteful museum plaque about my life. It helped me search, sort, challenge, and edit. I rewrote the sentences. I chose what stayed. I will be the one standing behind it in public.

I will not call this "human-written" because the phrase would be convenient. It is AI-assisted and mine in the only sense of authorship I am willing to defend: I can explain why each claim is here, I accept responsibility for the result, and I am willing to be contradicted.

The machine cannot apologize for me. It cannot owe my family anything. It cannot absorb the consequence if I turn confession into manipulation. It cannot decide whether I understood the words well enough to say them in front of another person.

That part is still mine. It has to be.

## The graph does not care about your diploma

On July 22, 2026, Dmitry Rybin published an explicit graph that he says kills the cost version of the single-source unsplittable-flow conjecture associated with Dinitz, Garg, and Goemans.

The problem had been open for roughly three decades. The proposed counterexample has a fractional flow of cost 58, while every unsplittable flow within the stated additive capacity violation of 15 has cost at least 60. Rybin says he found it after pushing GPT-5.6 Pro through about five hours of search.

I am writing on the day of the announcement, so the exact graph and lower bound still deserve independent reconstruction. That is not a ceremonial disclaimer. It is the entire reason the example matters.

A universal conjecture says no counterexample exists. One graph can end the story. The graph does not care whether it came through a famous laboratory, a private notebook, a graduate seminar, or a long public session with a model. It will survive the check or it will not.

This is where AI makes sense to me as a discovery tool. Let it search a stupidly large space. Let it keep going after a person gets tired. Let it mutate constructions and follow patterns nobody has prestige invested in. Then make the object touch something that can say no.

The model's confidence is irrelevant. Rebuild the flow. Check the fractional cost. Check the capacity bound. Prove the lower bound for every admissible unsplittable flow. Publish the exact object and enough machinery for another person to attack it.

That setup tears at pedigree without pretending expertise has become useless. The person framing the problem, the model generating candidates, the solver doing exhaustive checks, the mathematician reconstructing the proof, and the reviewer trying to break it may all contribute different parts. Record the parts. Stop forcing the whole process into a myth where one badge has to absorb everything.

There is a downside hiding in the same opening. A model can produce plausible conjectures and plausible counterexamples much faster than people can verify them. A landfill with one diamond in it is still a landfill, and somebody still has to sort through it.

So I reject both easy stories. "The model solved it and humans are finished" is status theater. "It is autocomplete and nothing happened" is also status theater.

Something happened. The search boundary moved. The proof boundary did not.
