## The robe and the farm

Khalil Gibran gave me one of the images that holds this argument together.

In *The Broken Wings*, Selma Karamy is not defeated because she fails to make the better case. There is no fair hearing available. The bishop controls the standard, benefits from the decision, and sits above meaningful appeal. His robe makes persuasion unnecessary. Power has already dressed itself as propriety, and everybody around it knows enough to understand what is happening while still behaving as though the proper process has taken place. That structure is the cage.

A system wears the robe when the judge writes the criterion, profits from the verdict, and prevents the people living under it from re-checking the decision. The costume changes. The structure does not have to.

A university can define merit, sell access to the definition, and point to the credential as evidence that the definition worked. A publisher can let prestige substitute for inspectable reasoning. A benchmark can keep handing out authority even when its tests are too weak to reject a wrong answer. A technology company can define safety, originality, capability, or acceptable use while keeping the evidence needed to challenge those definitions inside the same institution.

A platform can eat culture, own the route to the audience, generate substitutes for the people whose work fed it, set the price of visibility, and still present itself as the neutral marketplace.

Creative communities can wear the robe too. I do not say that to dismiss the real harm being done to artists. A human-only practice can matter. Process can be part of the meaning. Consent, labor, identity, chosen constraint, and tradition all matter. A community is allowed to say, "This space is for people working under these conditions."

The problem begins when pedigree is smuggled in under the name of ethics. Familiarity becomes authenticity. Difficulty becomes virtue. Membership becomes merit. The old gate feels human because people spent years learning how to survive inside it.

I wore the robe whenever I decided my intention mattered more than somebody else's experience. I wrote the criterion, benefited from the verdict, and made appeal expensive through anger, absence, charm, collapse, threat, or the sudden performance of being too broken to answer a plain question.

Hating the robe did not stop me from putting it on.

That matters because I have been anti-authoritarian for most of my life. I know the clean version of that story: I distrusted power, found the weak point, and built alternatives for people who had been kept outside. Sometimes that is true. Another version is that I hated being ruled and did not notice how often I was trying to become unruleable.

Pink Floyd gave me the sound of that contradiction before I had language for it. You could say I am asking too much of a record, and maybe I am, but *Animals* was the record that shaped a lot of my early criticism and wonder. I heard it before adolescence, before I knew what class analysis was, before I had a useful vocabulary for institutions or economic power. I understood the animals anyway.

I understood the dogs who keep running until pursuit has replaced purpose. I understood the pigs who turn appetite into moral authority. I understood the sheep who move with the group because the danger of leaving feels more immediate than the danger of where the group is going. The record sounded industrial and biological at the same time: concrete, machinery, isolation, instinct, hierarchy, and enormous human structures behaving as if they were simply nature.

It did not arrive as a lesson. That was important. It created a room in my mind and let the question move around in it. I could feel that society said one thing about itself while rewarding another set of behaviors. I could feel the distance between the language of civilization and the animal logic underneath it. The music made that distance beautiful enough to keep looking at. Orwell then gave me the farm. *Animal Farm* made a distinction I have had to relearn repeatedly: removing the owner does not necessarily remove ownership. A revolution can change the slogans, the names on the wall, and the people in the farmhouse while preserving the grammar of the relationship underneath. The rebel can inherit the robe. The animal who learned to recognize the farmer can still want the farmer's chair.

I absorbed that as political criticism long before I understood it as a personal warning.

At first, the animals were always other people. The dogs were executives, enforcers, climbers, and anyone willing to bite for proximity to power. The pigs were politicians, preachers, billionaires, administrators, and founders who converted coordination into ownership. The sheep were the people who followed, repeated, and accepted.

That reading left one category conveniently unoccupied: mine.

I remained the observer. The critic. The human being standing outside the enclosure, naming everybody correctly.

It took longer to admit that all three animals can occupy the same person, sometimes in the same afternoon.

I have been the dog. I have chased prestige, recognition, relief, capability, money, and the possibility of finally becoming impressive enough to quiet the private argument about whether I deserved to exist. I have pursued goals after the pursuit began harming the people walking beside me. I have mistaken movement for direction and intensity for sincerity.

I have been the pig. I have criticized hypocrisy while protecting my own. I have talked about liberation while expecting other people to absorb the consequences of my comfort and instability. I have treated perception as entitlement, as though seeing the cage clearly placed me above the obligation to examine what I was doing inside it.

I have been the sheep. I have followed inherited ideas about masculinity, success, substances, survival, resentment, and independence. I have called behavior self-determined merely because it moved against an authority, even when the authority still controlled the direction by giving me something to oppose.

If this sounds like I am turning an album into a personality test, that is not what I mean. The categories become dangerous when they harden into species. The dog is not born to hunt forever. The pig is not metaphysically corrupt. The sheep is not a lesser form of person. They are roles, patterns, and capacities placed under particular incentives.

Persistence can become predation when the target replaces the reason for running. Stewardship can become ownership when the coordinator begins believing that everything passing through the system belongs to the person maintaining it. Trust and social learning can become submission when the group loses the right to question where it is going.

The organic principle underneath each role is not necessarily the problem. The problem is what happens when a living capacity is made static, rewarded, and turned into a permanent position in a hierarchy. We do something similar with our isms: exchange becomes capitalism after ownership consumes the relationship. Agency becomes individualism after interdependence is treated as weakness. Direction becomes idealism after purity replaces movement. Care becomes propriety after permission replaces judgment. Self-determination becomes independence after responsibility to other people is described as an intrusion.

We take a living principle, place it in a jar, put a label on the glass, and eventually forget that the thing underneath was ever alive. The dystopian novels widened the same recognition for me. *1984* is partly about terror, but it is also about control of the record: if the institution owns what happened yesterday, then disagreement today becomes difficult to sustain. *Brave New World* understands that people do not always have to be beaten into submission. They can be distracted, comforted, stimulated, and supplied with enough immediate relief that freedom begins to feel like an unnecessarily difficult subject. *Fahrenheit 451* understands that a culture can lose its capacity for sustained thought before anyone has to formally outlaw thinking. Speed, noise, entertainment, and the pressure to react can do some of the work themselves.

These books are not interchangeable. A family argument is not a totalitarian state, a social platform is not Oceania, and addiction is not a Ministry. I do not want every disagreement dressed up as a dystopia because the comparison gives the speaker moral drama, but mechanisms can still rhyme across different scales: the record can be rewritten. Language can be narrowed. Comfort can become anesthesia. Urgency can become law. A person can make themselves difficult to appeal to while insisting they are the one being controlled, and that last one is familiar to me. I spent years inside a civil war between pride and shame. Pride said I was uniquely capable and therefore should not have to move at an ordinary pace. Shame said I was uniquely ruined and therefore ordinary repair could not possibly be enough. Victimhood explained why the world had done this to me. Predatory thinking answered that if the world was already corrupt, taking what I could was merely realism.

Those positions sound opposed, but they can serve the same private authority. They keep the entire frame centered on me.

The world really is beautiful. It is also full of structures and behaviors that strike deeply enough to make me feel sick. I do not want to lose that moral nausea. Some things should remain intolerable. But disgust is not a complete politics, and it is not a trustworthy guide by itself. I can hate exploitation and still exploit. I can recognize propaganda and still rewrite the private record. I can oppose the farm and still want the farmhouse.

Criticism means very little if it cannot turn around and look at me.

This is why I do not want a better bishop, a kinder pig, or a founder whose personality is persuasive enough that nobody asks for an appeal. I want the conditions to change. The criterion should not belong entirely to the person being judged by it or benefiting from it. The record should survive embarrassment. The people affected should have somewhere to stand when they disagree.

The work should be able to condemn the worker.
