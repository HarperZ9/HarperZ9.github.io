## What I owe the Mad-Happy Scientist

I keep returning to that child because I think a lot of people have some version of him.

Maybe yours was an artist, an astronaut, a mechanic, a naturalist, a writer, a musician, a teacher, a healer, or the kid who took apart every broken object in the house because they needed to know whether failure had a shape.

Then life happened. Some people jumped through the hoops and made it. Some tripped. Some were pushed out. Some turned left. Some found work that paid the rent and slowly separated them from the part of themselves that asked questions for no reason besides wonder.

I treated the missed road into formal research as a verdict. I let pedigree become metaphysics. The institution had a map, and I decided any territory outside it must be imaginary.

Then I resented the institution for a cage I was helping maintain.

That resentment fed my grandiosity. Every project had to prove everything. Every good week had to become an empire. Every new tool needed to repay every lost year immediately. The bipolar tendencies I recognize in myself can turn light into appetite. I find something real and want all of it: more capability, more projects, more proof, more impact, more recognition, every door open at once.

The structure outruns me. I collapse. People around me inherit the debris.

I have spent a lot of time trying to swallow the sun and acting surprised when it burns.

Grandiosity and shame are close relatives. One says I deserve the keys to the city. The other says I am too corrupt to enter. Both keep the world organized around me. Ordinary work starts feeling too small, which is convenient because ordinary work is where accountability usually lives.

One test that can fail. One claim another person can inspect. One tool that runs on a machine somebody already owns. One artist who names the lineage. One apology made without controlling the response. One boundary accepted even though it hurts. One door left open behind you.

None of those things feel large enough when I am trying to repay an entire life in a weekend, but they are still the things that count.

I still want the keys to the city. That hunger did not disappear because I found better language for it. If somebody handed me the keys, I would probably still notice the lock and wonder whether I could open it another way.

The change is that I do not want to pick the lock only for myself.

I want to open the cage, throw away the key, and let the walls rot into soil. I want somebody who never learned the password to have a surface where their work can be examined without first asking whether they belong. I want artists to have choices that are not limited to extraction or martyrdom. I want workers to keep some of the time the machine saves. I want people to use tools they can inspect and leave. I want a structure where the person maintaining the bridge cannot quietly claim both shores.

That sounds beautiful. It should also make you suspicious.

People promising universal liberation have worn a lot of robes. The founder who says he is building for everyone can become another bishop faster than he can say *open source*. A movement can begin by rejecting the old gate and end by deciding that disagreement itself is sabotage.

So contradict me. Inspect the system. Reject the doctrine. Fork the tool. Leave the platform. Protect your interior. Tell me where the argument breaks.

Nothing I build gets to inherit authority from the fact that I hate authority.

I used to want a killing blow against my ego. Even that can become theater. There is no clean terminal command for the self. The ego does not need root access, but I cannot delete it and call the deletion repair.

Self-destruction removes the person who owes the work.

At sixteen I thought disappearance might solve the contradiction of being alive inside myself. Later I found slower ways to disappear: drugs, fights, code, isolation, ambition, fantasy, collapse, and the performance of hating myself so thoroughly that nobody could ask for anything more specific.

I am done using annihilation as evidence that I understand the harm, which means the apology has to be less cinematic. Tell the truth before exposure forces it. Correct the record. Return what can be returned. Pay the cost you moved. Accept another person's boundary without turning it into a referendum on your worth. Listen without editing their account into something easier to survive. Do not demand forgiveness as the prize for finally admitting what happened. Put friction around behavior that good intentions have repeatedly failed to stop.

Then continue, because repair needs somebody alive to do it.

My mistakes are part of the record. They inform the risk. They do not get to write every future verdict alone.

I am not asking to be seen as good. I am trying to become answerable.

Maybe that is the human quality I was looking for beneath all of this. Not a protected list of tasks a machine can never do. The ability to owe. To be contradicted. To change without deleting the record. To care what our actions do inside another life. To remain present after the explanation runs out.

## The promise I can actually make

I believe this strongly enough that I am afraid of failing it.

Even after writing all of this, I do not trust myself to live by every word for the rest of my life. I know my patterns. I know how conviction can burn hot and still lose to illness, appetite, exhaustion, shame, fear, or the next idea that makes everything else disappear. I am not going to clean that doubt out of the ending just to make the ending feel complete.

Mental health belongs here because no system built for people gets to pretend people are stable machines. Everybody needs some way back to themselves. Faith. Meditation. Therapy. Medication. Curiosity. Reflection. Movement. Art. Community. Maybe one of those. Maybe several. Maybe the combination changes over time.

I do not do especially well with one-on-one therapy. I still prefer self-medication more often than I should. That is not advice, and it is not a theory of freedom. It is part of the record.

Sometimes avoiding an institution is self-knowledge. Sometimes it is avoidance wearing the clothes of self-determination. A path your spirit did not choose can become another cage. A self-chosen path can still walk you straight into a wall.

Assistance is not surrender. Autonomy is not immunity.

The question I have to ask is whether the route makes me more present, less destructive, and more able to answer for what I do. If it only helps me disappear more comfortably, it is not freedom simply because I picked it.

I have said I like to sit in my pain. The more honest answer is that I do a bit of both. Sometimes I sit beside it because I need to hear what it has been trying to tell me. Sometimes I sit inside it because punishment feels safer than change. I have made a home there before. I know the furniture.

I own that. I am also beginning to learn how to love myself again.

I believe people deserve the possibility of redemption. Not erasure, and not automatic forgiveness. Redemption means the record does not have to become the rest of a life. If only clean people qualify, it is just another gate.

Sometimes the bridge over the canyon fails and you go tumbling down. You do not wake up back on the road. You still have to get up, however slowly, and make the march out. Maybe you crawl for part of it. Maybe somebody walks beside you. Maybe the first day of progress looks almost exactly like lying still, except you have finally stopped digging.

Otherwise you become a memory of yesterday while you are still alive.

I think about *The Green Mile* and the fantasy of taking pain out of another person, holding it long enough to change its direction, and leaving something gentler behind. I understand why that image stayed with me. I spent a lot of my life taking pain in, mixing it with my own, and throwing it back into the room in a worse shape.

I do not want martyrdom to become another escape. Nobody should have to absorb the whole world's pain until they disappear. The gift has to be more durable than sacrifice: a thought, a tool, a structure, a place where the next person does not have to carry the same weight alone.

So, to younger people and to anybody else who recognizes part of themselves here, do not take me as an example of what to do. I repeated mistakes people before me made. I made the same excuses and then found new language and better tools for making them. I took what I received from family, peers, relationships, music, institutions, and every room that taught me how to survive. I internalized it, distorted it, and sometimes handed it to somebody else.

My behavior reflects that environment. It is not a complete reflection of my intentions or the world I believe should exist. It is still my behavior. Environment explains a route. It does not walk the route for me.

Environment is the medium. It shapes a person, an animal, an idea, a piece of art, a song, a wound, a criticism. It feeds some tendencies and starves others. That is not an acquittal. It is the reason transformation is possible.

Change the conditions. Preserve the record. Make another choice. Something else may grow.

Until a life ends, another move remains. Even after a life ends, the impact continues moving through other people. A person may cause damage and still leave behind a lesson that prevents somebody else from repeating it. That does not turn the damage into good. It means the record can still serve life.

I believe in people who cannot currently believe in themselves. I am trying to extend the same belief toward me without turning it into permission.

I do not feel good enough to leave a gift. I may never feel good enough. Fine. The gift does not need my self-image to become useful. The work is mixed and matched. Some pieces do not fit. Some parts fail. I am still dialing it in. It is strange, like me. The principle matters enough to keep working anyway.

I want this to become a platform where people from different lives, postures, kinds of damage, and ways of seeing can understand one another without pretending the differences are fake. We all arrive crying. We do not receive the same world, and we are not equally protected from it. Still, almost everybody is only a few decisions, losses, rooms, or missing hands away from the person beside them they judge.

I am not announcing an exit. I am here. I stand behind the idea. I am putting it down because anything I want to survive a mood has to leave my head. Record it. Archive it. Let people argue with it. Let them repair it. Then turn it toward something material: healing, learning, art, discovery, food, rent, time, and people being paid enough to care for the people they love.

Desperation is not a character test. People should not have to become cruel or ridiculous to survive because somebody inserted themselves between the work and the meal.

The middleman can be a regular person too. A steward at the table instead of the owner of it. We can make the thing, share the gain, and leave enough behind for somebody else to begin.

I once said that an intention to do good, in its most sincere form, can never be tarnished. I know what I meant, but I also have to hold that sentence to the same standard as everything else here: sincerity is not a receipt. A good intention can be contradicted by behavior. It can be turned into a robe. It can become the story I tell so I do not have to look at the effect. Maybe the intention remains sincere somewhere underneath. That is not enough.

The gift begins when sincerity survives contact with somebody else's reality and becomes something they can use without having to trust me.

That is what I want to leave behind.

I cannot promise perfect behavior. That would be another lie dressed as a promise.

What I can promise is that I stand by the conviction. I can construct a thought and a structure today that I will not tear down tomorrow merely because I fail to live up to it. I can refuse to rewrite the principle to protect myself. I can leave the receipt and give other people the right to remind me what I said.

I want to build this for the people who endure, for the people we lost, and for the people who may not know whether they can endure what comes next. Not as a monument to suffering. As a handrail. A place to put weight down. A structure that does not require everybody to be strong at the same time.

Maybe I fail the conviction, contradict it, or leave somebody else to carry it farther than I can. The structure should survive that, and it should survive me.
