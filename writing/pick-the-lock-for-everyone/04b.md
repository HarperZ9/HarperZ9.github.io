## A steward, not a thief

There is another system I keep thinking about because criticism alone does not feed anybody.

Artists use references. They build moodboards, make collages, save images, trade folders, follow chains of influence, and sometimes discover a visual language through a hundred small encounters they could not reconstruct later if they tried. None of that began with AI. The difference now is scale, opacity, and the ability of a company to swallow the whole chain while leaving every earlier person outside the transaction.

I think we can build a different supply chain.

Imagine a creative workspace where a person can bring in a moodboard or a collection of references and the system keeps a living source graph. Not a plagiarism detector pretending influence is a crime. Not a surveillance machine scoring which dead painter owns twelve percent of a brushstroke. A practical record of the things the artist actually chose to place in the room: this photograph, that poster, this palette, that composition, a type treatment, a shader, a sound, a texture, a collaborator's sketch.

The earlier creator stays attached to the object instead of being erased the second it enters somebody else's process. Their name, license, preferred credit, source link, and terms can travel with it. If the final work earns money, the people in that declared chain can receive a share when the relationship is clear enough and the participants agree to it.

The analogy in my head is a modlist that can assemble a world without pretending it authored every mod inside it. Coordination adds real value. It does not have to erase the people whose work made coordination possible.

This cannot become another automated tax on inspiration. Influence is not ownership, and human memory is not a clean dependency graph. A system that demands payment every time two shapes resemble each other would be worse than the thing it replaced. The point is to make declared sourcing easier, to preserve consent where consent can exist, and to let artists negotiate relationships instead of forcing every conflict through a platform policy written by somebody who profits from both sides.

The dispute surface matters as much as the payment rail. An artist should be able to say, "That is my work, this use crossed my boundary," and reach an actual person. Another artist should be able to answer, show the process, correct the record, offer credit, remove the source, negotiate a license, or disagree without the entire exchange becoming a public execution.

Maybe some of those arguments end in collaboration. Maybe two communities that currently meet only through accusation discover that they like each other's work. Maybe the moodboard becomes an invitation instead of a graveyard of stripped context.

That is the kind of platform I would rather build: rendering tools, native tools, generative systems, abstraction tools, epistemic engines, and accountability infrastructure living in the same openbox environment, with the community able to inspect the rules and change them.

The platform should not own the artist's audience, identity, work, or relationship with another artist. It should not insert itself forever because it helped two people find each other once. It should not take thirty percent because a database row moved. If it charges anything, the fee should be visible, bounded, and tied to work the service actually performed. The treasury can be governed by the people using and maintaining the system. The code, records, and social graph should be portable enough that leaving is a real option.

A middleman can be useful. The problem begins when coordination becomes ownership.

A steward keeps the table working. A thief locks the pantry and rents the food back.

The same principle applies outside art. I want service platforms whose success is measured partly by how much time they return to the person using them.

Not how many minutes they captured. Not how many notifications they generated. Not whether the saved hour was converted into another hour of output for somebody else.

Did the user get their time back, and were they allowed to keep it?

That is the time dividend.

If a service turns a week of paperwork into an afternoon, the person should receive the remaining days as life. Rest. Care. Study. Making something. Sitting with their family. Going outside. Doing absolutely nothing. The system does not get to call that unused capacity and sell it again.

We could even make the claim inspectable. What did the service do? What did it automate? How much human labor did it actually remove? What new review burden did it create? Who received the saved time? Who received the money? Who absorbed the risk when it failed?

A platform that saves ten hours and gives the user two while turning eight into margin did not return time. It extracted a productivity gain and wrote liberation on the invoice.

This is where cooperative infrastructure stops being a sentimental extra. It becomes the mechanism that decides whether abundance is shared or hoarded. The people supplying the art, maintenance, moderation, compute, care, and judgment need standing in the system that allocates the gain.

I do not know the final economic model. I know what it cannot be: another landlord sitting between people who were already capable of helping one another.

Maybe we can all eat if we stop hoarding the food.
