# Pick the Lock for Everyone

## An apology, an argument about AI, and a refusal to keep mistaking the cage for the world

*Zain Dana Harper, July 2026*

> Content note: suicidal depression, dissociation, substance use, violence, shame, and harm to other people. There are no descriptions of methods.

I have never apologized.

I have said sorry plenty of times. I have explained myself until the explanation became the only thing in the room. I have hated myself loudly enough that somebody else had to stop being angry and start taking care of me. I have disappeared, gotten fucked up, started a new project, found a new enemy, blamed capitalism, blamed trauma, blamed timing, blamed mental illness, blamed the people around me, blamed the world for failing to recognize what I could have been.

Some of those explanations were true. That almost made them worse. A true explanation can still be used like a fake passport.

When I was sixteen I was suicidal, depressed, dissociating, acting out, and swallowing nearly everything I felt until it came back out sideways. I have almost died from drugs, fights, self-criticism, and plain reckless bullshit. I have hurt people physically, mentally, and spiritually. I cheated on tests. I made video game cheats. I lied my way around consequences. I learned how to find the loose board in the fence and called that intelligence.

I became very good at locating the gap.

Give me a game and I would look for the memory address. Give me a rule and I would look for the part nobody enforced. Give me a test and I would look for the shortcut. Give me a relationship, let shame get close enough, and I would start searching for the exit before the other person could finish telling me what I had done.

Then I would go back to my room. Drink. Program. Make graphics tools. Modify games. Chase some weird technical question until four in the morning. Build another little world where I wrote the laws and, conveniently, passed every test.

The kid in that room wanted to become what I called the Mad-Happy Scientist.

That was the dream before I knew what a career was. I wanted to make instruments. I wanted graphics, code, mathematics, biology, physics, the whole strange moving mess of it. I wanted to ask questions that sounded stupid until something answered back. I wanted to be wrong in an interesting direction. I wanted to find the thing under the thing.

I did not want a title. I did not dream about prestige, networking, or a fucking LinkedIn banner. I wanted wonder, and I let myself lose it while pretending I was still chasing it.

I took a different road, then spent years treating that turn as a permanent sentence. I kept research and art alive as a moonlighting operation. Every late-night repository became evidence that I had not really abandoned the dream. Every unfinished tool was a promissory note to the future version of me who would finally get serious. Every burst of energy became the start of the life I was "supposed" to have, and when the burst collapsed I used the wreckage to prove I had failed again.

I made private checkpoints no person could satisfy. Then I missed them. Then I called the miss destiny.

My family and the people I love paid for that. They paid in money, sleep, worry, neglect, unfinished promises, and the exhausting question of whether the light I found this week was going to become something real or another fire I expected them to stand beside while I burned through myself.

So I am trying to apologize, first to that kid.

I am sorry I abandoned you and then used your name as cover. I am sorry I made curiosity into a weapon I could turn against myself. I am sorry I kept telling you we were almost there while walking in circles and blaming the walls.

I am sorry to the people I love for making them carry a war I would not name. For demanding faith while refusing consistency. For acting like my pain was the only evidence that mattered.

And this essay is not the apology they are owed. That has to happen with no audience and no control over the answer. They get to be angry. They get to refuse me. They get to remember things differently. They do not owe me absolution because I finally managed to put a few ugly sentences in public.

A confession can be another shortcut. I know because shortcuts are my field.

## The black box I know best

Everybody keeps talking about black-box AI as though opacity showed up with neural networks.

Come on.

People are black boxes with excellent public relations. We make a decision, feel the decision, then hire language to defend it. We call appetite principle. We call fear discernment. We call status merit. We call property ethics. We call obedience professionalism. We call avoidance self-care. We call a legal outcome moral because the law happened to protect the person already holding the keys.

I did the same thing on a smaller surface, at home, where it hurt people I claimed to love.

When I lied, I was not moving a few words around. I was editing somebody else's picture of reality without their permission and then benefiting from what they did inside the picture I gave them. When I cheated, I was changing the evidence other people used to decide who had done the work, who understood it, who deserved trust, who got the opportunity.

I controlled the input, then acted offended when anybody questioned the output.

That is a tiny autocracy. Nothing glamorous about it. I hated authority and wore a little crown in private.

This is also why I do not trust anti-authoritarianism by itself. "No" can save a life. A kid says no to an abusive parent. A worker says no to exploitation. An artist says no when a company wants their voice, face, style, or labor for free. A citizen says no to a state that has confused force with legitimacy.

I also know another kind of no. The one that appears when somebody else has a boundary and I cannot stand the feeling of not being sovereign.

I still lie. I still avoid. I still have the ability to charm my way around the part where I should sit down, shut up, and listen. I am still capable of turning a real wound into a license to wound somebody else. Writing that here does not make me honest. It is closer to a risk disclosure.

There is a temptation to say, "I am a liar and a cheat," as if the identity closes the case. It can become one more hiding place. If I am rotten all the way through, then nothing specific can be repaired. Repetition becomes fate. Shame gets to feel profound while doing absolutely no work.

The useful sentence is uglier because it leaves me with a job: I have lied. I have cheated. I am capable of doing both again. I need structures around the part of me that starts hunting for a shortcut when the cost can be pushed onto somebody else.
