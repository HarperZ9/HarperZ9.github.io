## Build the instrument, not the imitation

The strongest moral criticism of current generative media is not that computation touched art. Artists have used cameras, darkrooms, plotters, samplers, synthesizers, procedural systems, 3D software, compositing, game engines, custom shaders, found material, assistants, and fabrication shops for a long time.

The tool analogy does not erase the scale or training history of foundation models. It only reminds us that creative agency has never meant performing every operation with bare hands.

The sharper question is what entered the generator, under whose permission, and who captured the value. Someone will answer, "Artists have always borrowed." Yes, they have, and that is exactly why scale, consent, attribution, and power need to stay in the conversation rather than being flattened into a purity test. Current foundation models were shaped through enormous corpora that included copyrighted and culturally situated work. The legal analysis is not one universal yes or no, and I am not trying to settle it in an essay. The moral conflict is easier to see. Millions of people were never offered a meaningful choice before their work became part of a private capability that may compete with them.

I think true generative art can be built on another foundation.

Start lower than the prompt. Build the instrument.

The artist chooses the primitives: points, curves, glyphs, meshes, particles, colors, marks, materials, sounds, fields. Then the artist chooses the laws that move them: grammars, collision rules, growth, reaction-diffusion, cellular systems, fluid approximations, signal processing, shaders, topology, custom physics, whatever the work needs.

Variation can come from a seed, a hand gesture, a drawing, a microphone, a camera used as a sensor rather than a cultural scraper, local weather, motion, a plant, a machine, the electrical noise of the room, or material a collaborator deliberately contributes.

Search can be evolutionary. It can use novelty search, program synthesis, constraint solving, reinforcement learning inside the world the artist built, or a person hammering on the system for months until they begin to understand its temperament.

The artist is not asking a hidden archive to imitate a finished surface. They are constructing a possibility space, entering it, and learning what the rules made possible by accident.

That can be art. The instrument itself may be the deepest part of the work.

When I think about *Jacob's Ladder*, *Platoon*, Dali, Beksinski, or the images that have followed this essay, the honest question is not how to make a machine copy the surface. It is what reached me underneath: dissonance, sacrifice, impossible space, a body becoming a shell, a threshold that feels both like escape and judgment, light caught in a ruined world, a thread still pulling toward something the person cannot name.

Those relations can become forces, constraints, materials, timing, geometry, and transformation. That is different from asking for something "in the style of" another person. It is a system built to explore the pressure underneath the style.

A learned system can also begin from random initialization and train only on self-authored material, licensed collaborators, public-domain sources, synthetic simulation, or interaction inside a bounded environment. Weights are not morally cursed. The question is what formed them, under whose permission, and whether the account is honest.

Another artist may choose a foundation model and disclose it. Fine. Say so. Declare the ancestry and let the audience decide what that means.

I do not want a new purity hierarchy. I want real choices and a lineage somebody can inspect.

Sampling is the analogy I keep returning to, though it needs to be handled carefully.

Sampling did not prove musicians had run out of ideas. It revealed that culture was already recursive. A drum break could leave one room, cross a decade, meet a machine, a neighborhood, another grief, and become something that did not exist before. The sampler was not the whole act. Selection mattered. Timing mattered. Chopping, arrangement, context, performance, and the person hearing a possibility inside the earlier sound mattered.

The ugliest part was not transformation itself; it was the ownership system around the masters: labels, clearance regimes, contracts, distribution choke points, and people making themselves indispensable by standing between artists and one another. AI is rebuilding the record label around the sampler, and that is what I want to interrupt.

The model can become an instrument. A source graph can preserve lineage. Artists can declare what entered, what was licensed, what was refused, what was transformed, and who should receive credit or an agreed share. Communities can own the tools, the relationships, and the treasury, while a steward coordinates without becoming the landlord of the culture moving through the system. Keep the lineage and lose the label. That principle applies beyond art. Every domain has its record labels: journals that confuse distribution with authority, credentials that confuse access with merit, banks that confuse coordination with ownership, cloud platforms that confuse hosting with a permanent claim on capability, and bureaucracies that turn a necessary boundary into a hereditary class of people allowed to speak.

The point is not to erase expertise, consent, attribution, privacy, or payment. The point is to stop allowing the coordinator to own the entire reaction.

A serious creative tool could write a process manifest as it works. It could record what entered, what stayed out, which parts learned, how candidates were explored, when a human intervened, what compute was used, and what rights attached to the material. It could keep failed paths and replay the sequence that produced the final artifact.

The receipt would not certify originality. Nothing comes from nowhere. Human artists digest parents, teachers, enemies, neighborhoods, books, accidents, other artists, software, weather, music through a wall, and the awful sentence somebody said ten years ago that never stopped echoing.

Novelty is not an absence of ancestry. It is a relation that did not exist before, made through an ancestry we are willing to name.

This is where abstraction becomes useful. A picture stops being only a picture and becomes marks, forces, timing, constraints, choices, and perception. A brush becomes one implementation of a mark-making system. A model becomes one implementation of search. Once the concrete thing is understood as a relation, we can rebuild the relation without pretending the inherited implementation was nature.

Abstraction can erase people too. Capital does this constantly. A worker becomes throughput. Culture becomes training data. A community becomes a market segment. A person becomes inventory.

So I care about what survives the abstraction: agency, consent, relationship, ecological cost, the ability to refuse, and the ability to appeal. If only the monetizable part survives, we did not discover a new world. We found a cleaner interface for the old violence.
