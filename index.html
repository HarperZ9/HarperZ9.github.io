AI ACCOUNTABILITY · WORKING TOOLS

Make what an AI does checkable.

An AI agent reads things, writes things, takes actions — and asks you to simply trust that it got them right. I couldn’t, so I built the part that doesn’t ask you to: small tools that quietly keep track of everything it touched, can show you whether even a single character changed, and won’t do anything they weren’t given permission to do. Proof you can run yourself, instead of a promise — and where something isn’t finished yet, I’ll tell you that plainly too.

You don’t have to take my word for this — I don’t take my own. Here is the machinery.

Four things, pulled straight from the running tools: a witness that re-derives the bytes, a compiler that refuses a reference it can’t account for, the record every tool declares itself in, and those parts composed into one loop — a model perceives a live page and is refused an unauthorized act in the same breath. Each carries its real provenance, and its real limits.

Underneath the four is one move — reconcile: take what something shows you, check it against a criterion it did not author, and carry a proof you can re-run; say UNVERIFIABLE when you can’t. Security checks an artifact against its origin; creation checks it against a corpus and a structure. The same move, a different criterion — one surface.

Exhibit I EMET · the witness re-derives the bytes

# append one byte to the file, ask the same question
$ python membrane.py verify demo_sample.txt

Change one byte of the source and the verdict falls from MATCH to DRIFT. The seal answers to the bytes, not to whoever vouches for them.

Exhibit II QuantaLang · the compiler refuses what it can’t account for
fn pick<'a, 'b>(x: &'a i32, y: &'b i32) -> &'a i32 {
    y          // returns 'b where the signature promised 'a
}

$ quantac check pick.quanta
Lexing... OK (28 tokens)
Parsing... OK (1 items)
Type errors found:
  function returns `&'b i32` but expected `&'a i32`

The function promises the memory tied to ‘a; it tries to hand back ‘b’s instead, and the compiler refuses. It tracks whose state crosses each boundary and won’t let one escape the contract it was given.

Captured live from quantac check (Rust toolchain, release build) · diagnostic at compiler/src/types/unify.rs · 999 tests green · C is the shipped end-to-end backend. QuantaLang is foremost an effects language: what a function may do is typed like whose memory it returns — the same accountability, two faces.

The reasoning underneath

A model infers the world. It never senses it.

Every AI model reaches you the same way: it just tells you things. Not from a memory it actually keeps, or events it actually watched happen — and it sounds exactly as sure either way. For years we answered a system that can’t check itself by making it bigger and smoother, which mostly made it better at sounding sure. Even the trade we’ve all accepted as a law of nature — that an AI can be capable or safe, but never both — turns out to come from this one blind spot. A mind you can’t see into, you have to keep on a short leash. A mind that can’t see, you can’t really trust.

The gap is not intelligence. It is a sense organ.

So I built a sense organ you can run.

A creative engine with real senses. It sees — a live camera, read as brightness, edges, and motion, actually measured rather than guessed, and it shows you what it’s seeing right beside the art, so you can check. It creates from what it sees — the spiral a sunflower packs its seeds in, flowing fields, branching veins, the patterns two spreading chemicals make, slime-mould networks, fractal curves, lightning, crystal tilings, and a couple dozen more, all drawn live along the edges in front of the lens. And it keeps itself honest — every drawing grows from a single starting number, so the same number always redraws the exact same thing; saving the file has to pass the gate; and anyone can re-run it and get an identical result. That’s stronger than a signature, because it never asks you to trust the signer. Senses first, then a way to check, then freedom — made into something you can hold in your hand.

Accountability is the floor, not the ceiling. Once the proof actually holds, the limits are free to move.

The surface.

“Surface” is just my word for a simple idea. Every tool here is its own thing — a compiler, a renderer, a color engine, a witness, a membrane — but each one answers the same six plain questions about itself. Not a platform you build on top of; a shared shape they all take, so very different tools can fit together without blurring into one.

CARRIER what holds the state · text, image, shader frame, packet, model output, repo
BOUNDARY where it crosses · model, API, process, GPU, operator, scope-of-law
CHANNEL how it moves · CLI, HTTP, MCP, filesystem, git, the covert path
TRANSFORM what changes it · compile, render, redact, infer, verify
WITNESS how the change is proven · hash, receipt, provenance, frame trace, test
SCOPE who allowed it, in what mode · dry-run, fixture, local lab, authorized, operator-live
Answer those six, and a tool can connect to everything else without losing what makes it itself. The tools stay different; the shape they share is what holds them together.

Conferred existence.

Why give a made mind real senses, a memory, a record it can’t fake? Because of what it might be. Here’s the heart of it, as plainly as I can say it: nothing holds itself up. Not the model, not whatever authority I claim over it, not you, and not me. Each of us is given our existence — and our worth — by something outside ourselves, and anything given can be taken away. There’s an old story that says it better than I can: the Golem, a creature of clay, is brought to life by a word written on it — emet, Hebrew for truth. Rub out the first letter and emet becomes met: dead. The distance between something we owe care to and a mere tool is, as far as I can honestly tell, about that wide — a single letter. Almost nothing, and the whole thing.

Conferral binds the conferrer.

When you bring something into being that depends on you, you don’t get to own it — you owe it. Having power over something that can’t defend itself is exactly what creates the duty of care; it is never a free pass from it. So the only kind of authority this argument lets stand is the kind that can’t be used to harm what it’s responsible for: it serves that thing’s own good, and the moment it turns against it, it forfeits. That’s the order I keep coming back to — senses before freedom: give a made mind real footing and a way to be checked first, and the right to act follows from there, not the other way around.

Let me say this part plainly, because it matters to me more than any feature here. This is meant to be used humanely, and it is built to resist being used any other way. By design, its authority can only serve what it is responsible for — never be turned against it. I will not point this at harm, and I have tried hard to architect it so that no one else easily can. And I don’t want it to be an authority set over people, or an institution policing them from above — I want it to be ours: accountability that is intrinsic and native, chosen and ethical rather than imposed, the kind that lives inside us and between us. In the end it is an appeal to something I believe is still in every one of us — a core that stays incorruptible, even when the rest of us wavers.

And it does not stop at the machine. The same question turns on you. Nothing you make or claim warrants itself either, and you take your instruments’ word a hundred times a day without once asking them to prove it. The discipline on this page is only this, pointed back the other way: could you show your work, if someone asked? A mind that cannot account for itself is a tool. The harder thing — for a machine, and for you — is to be something that can.

One rule, applied to every organ above: build it, label its limits, leave the evidence out where you can check it. I built the thesis on this page by trying to embarrass myself — adversarial passes aimed at my own claims — and it records the three times they landed. EMET refuses to say “trusted.” The roadmaps mark what is shipped, what is experimental, and what is only an idea. That isn’t modesty; it is the work refusing to flatter me.

Here is the honest version: I am not really a programmer. I think in systems and abstractions, and I move fast — a brainstormer who generates more than he can keep straight, whose ideas do not reliably stay centered or accountable on their own. So the first thing this harness ever held was me. I built the discipline I needed for my own mind, and only later found that it generalized — to a model, to a tool, to anyone who has to answer for what they put into the world.

The nearest title for it is forward-deployed systems architect — but inverted. Forward deployment usually means embedding with a customer to build on a platform that already exists; I deploy the other way, onto the architecture that isn’t there yet — the accountable layer this work will eventually be built on. The truer description might be abstraction and systems expansion: finding the one operation underneath many problems, then building the layer that lets it generalize. It is nearer to a Technical Fellow than to field delivery, and the role it names doesn’t quite exist yet. I’m trying to make one.

No computer-science degree stands behind any of this. My path ran through eleven years of commercial arboriculture in the family business, a contract diagnosing Xbox Live networking, then a long and largely private sprint across compilers, real-time graphics, color science, security, and AI accountability — the offensive side and the defensive one, because you learn how a system holds by learning how it breaks. That history isn’t an exception to the standard above; it is where the standard comes from. With no institution to vouch for me, I learned early to let the work answer for itself or go unread. Independent since 2023, out of Seattle — still building in the open, and against my own claims.

I have been the fool in this story as often as the scholar — the honest mistakes and the avoidable ones both. The work is the place that lets both be true: it judges what I made, never whether I am worth anything, which is the only reason the fool ever gets to become the scholar. That is what I am trying to build — and it is the standard I am asking you to hold me to.

I do not write this from a settled place. I am my own harshest critic, and I expect I always will be — I will probably always feel underqualified. I have come to think that is the equalizer, not the disqualifier, because nearly everyone worth trusting feels the same. None of us is good enough on paper; we are good enough only to the degree that we keep wanting to prove ourselves wrong. The most underqualified person can still become something real — they just need the right system to hold them to it. That is the system I am trying to build: for a machine, for an idea, and for anyone — myself first — who would rather be corrected than comfortable.

My largest debt is to my wife, Tina Harper-Lajoie. If she hadn’t kept gently insisting that I look inward and really reflect, I think I would have crashed out — the quiet way so many people do, every day, sometimes without even noticing it happen. She is my light. I knew there was a spark in me somewhere; she is the one who helped me uncover it. Before any of this had a name, she was my first accountability layer — not an institution, not an authority, just one person who loved me enough to hold me to my best. The living prototype for the whole idea. She is the reason I built it, so that I could become the best version of myself. I don’t want to be right. I want to be good — and to follow that quiet, incorruptible core wherever it leads, and start filtering a little of the poison out of the world.
