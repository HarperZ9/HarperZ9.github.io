What I build

Accountability tools for AI-assisted work.

01
The core problem

Agents act fast. Review doesn't keep up.

As AI-assisted work speeds up, the bottleneck shifts to verification: proving who authorized what, which evidence backed which claim, and what state a system was in when it acted. A model can generate a plausible-looking report in seconds. Checking whether that report matches the source bytes takes a human and a set of tools that most teams don't have yet. I build those tools.

byte-level witnessesprovenance recordsrelease gateshandoff reports
02
The approach

Small, tested, inspectable. No magic.

The tools are narrow on purpose. Each one does one thing, has a test suite, and says clearly what it cannot do. EMET re-derives a hash and reports MATCH, DRIFT, or UNVERIFIABLE — that's the whole lattice, by design. The goal is a stack where every claim has evidence underneath it and every limit is stated plainly.

stdlib-only where possibleexplicit caveatsopen sourceno hidden state

Research thesis

Safety, transparency, and creativity should evolve on the same surface.

The same gap that makes AI dangerous also makes it feel limited: models infer state from assertions, memory, and context instead of sensing the world natively. My work is aimed at that meeting point: bilateral provenance, programmatic live-state organs, byte-level witnesses, and contract layers where people and machines can make work inspectable. That thesis shows up in EMET as the byte-witness spine, proof-surface as the shared contract, provenance-sensorium for live-state receipts, agent-audit for intent/action review, and QuantaLang for typed effects and CPU/GPU language research. No trust verdict, no magic authority: trusted context and scope travel inward, and a verifiable work record travels back out.
