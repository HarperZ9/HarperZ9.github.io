DEMONSTRATIONS · RUN IT YOURSELF

Don’t take the claim. Run it.

Two of the most important pieces are running right here on this page, so you can try them yourself. The witness takes a fingerprint of whatever you type — a real SHA-256, computed in your own browser — and tells you one of three things: MATCH (nothing changed), DRIFT (something changed), or UNVERIFIABLE (nothing to compare against yet). The gate runs the real decision logic and turns away anything it can’t be sure about. Change what you give them and watch the answer change. I would rather you check than take my word — I don’t take my own.

These are honest copies of the real, tested tools — the originals are linked under each one.

Real SHA-256 · the real verdict logic · nothing here emits trusted

The witness — re-derive, don’t trust.

Try it. Type something, then seal it — the witness takes a fingerprint of those exact words and remembers it. (A fingerprint here just means a short code that any change, even a single character, would scramble.) Now change one letter and witness again: the fresh fingerprint no longer matches the one it kept, so the answer is DRIFT — something changed. Clear the seal and there is nothing to compare against, so the answer is UNVERIFIABLE — not a pass, not a fail, just honestly unknown. That is the whole idea, and it is a small one: rather than trust that the words held, the witness checks for itself, and tells you plainly what it found.

UNVERIFIABLE
No seal yet — seal the content to give the witness something to re-derive against.

This demo needs JavaScript and the Web Crypto API. The real witness ships in EMET, linked below.

The fingerprinting is the real thing — crypto.subtle.digest("SHA-256"), a tool built right into your browser, run on what you typed and never sent anywhere. EMET does the same across whole files in three languages — 19/19 tests, github.com/HarperZ9. Honest limit: a plain seal like this proves the words weren’t changed by accident; it cannot stop a determined faker who simply makes a fresh fingerprint to match their new version. For that, the seal has to be kept somewhere separate — and when it can’t be sure, it says UNVERIFIABLE rather than fake a pass.

The gate — allow is earned, not assumed.

An action shows up at the gate, hoping to go ahead. The gate says no by default, and when anything is unclear it still says no: no permission slip means deny; anything it can’t be sure about becomes needs-human (stop and ask a person); and allow comes back only when every single thing checks out. Flip the conditions below and watch the answer move. Try to earn an allow — and notice just how much has to be true at the same time.

The request’s conditions
ALLOW
Every check passes — authority is stated, scoped, timed, budgeted, and grounded in observed state.

This is the real decision logic: a single no settles it; anything unknown becomes needs-human; and an allow needs a clean sweep, everything passing at once. The tested original lives in proof-surface — plain Python, nothing extra to install, 258 passing tests, github.com/HarperZ9/proof-surface. Honest limit: the gate only gives a recommendation — the program around it does the enforcing. This page can never grant a yes on its own.

Both, in one loop.

The witness and the gate are two stops on one loop — perceive → gate → act → verify → witness. In plain terms: first it looks, and what it sees has to pass its own honesty check or it stops. Then an action has to clear the gate, or it’s turned away. Then, after acting, it looks again and checks whether what happened matches what it meant to do — and if things drifted, it undoes them. No step just takes the step before it at its word; each one checks for itself. I built it this way because I needed it for my own thinking first — I couldn’t trust my own ideas to stay honest without something that made every step show its work. That whole living thing is accountable-surface.

A tool that claims to keep things accountable, while quietly handing itself power, has already broken its own promise — so nothing here does. Could you show your work, the same way, if someone asked?
