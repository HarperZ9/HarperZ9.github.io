ATELIER · ALGORITHMIC DRAWINGS

Living structure, drawn by algorithm.

iamBeTa photographs small living things up close — the spiral of a snail’s shell, the curl of a spent seedhead, the veins of a mallow flower. Here, each of those shapes becomes the starting point for a small program that draws: how leaves spiral around a stem, how fluid swirls, how a leaf grows its veins, how patterns ripple across a surface. Every line you see is drawn fresh right here in your browser — nothing on this page is a saved picture.

The seed is just a short starting code, and the same seed always draws the exact same thing. I didn’t want art you have to take on my word, so I built it to answer for itself: run it, steer it with a photograph, save the drawing — and it carries proof of where it came from, right inside the file.

In-browser · zero dependencies · deterministic from seed · export to a witnessed, plot-optimised SVG

The instrument.

Pick a drawing style and a photograph. The program reads the photo as a map of light and dark, and bends to follow it — so the snail’s spiral or the mallow’s veins start to show up in the strokes. Slide the complexity up or down, roll a new seed, swap the pens. When you find one you love, save it. It comes out as a clean file made for a drawing machine — a pen plotter — with strokes that touch joined into smooth, continuous lines, ordered so the pen lifts off the paper as little as possible, and sorted by pen color. Then it’s stamped with a witness — think of it as a tamper-proof fingerprint of the exact drawing. Anyone can re-run the file from its seed and get the identical drawing, and the file even notes, in its own fine print, what it tidied up. The web address holds that same recipe too — press Copy link and whoever opens it re-draws your exact picture, with no file to take on faith. And saving isn’t just a download button — it’s the action in a careful little loop. A gate (a checkpoint that says no by default) first makes sure the drawing has fully settled before it allows the file to be written, and only then does the witness stamp it. Look → check → act → sign — the same care, turned on the instrument’s own work.

Or bring your own picture entirely: press ↑ Upload to load a photograph, or pick Live · camera and the instrument sees through your webcam in real time — just what it actually measures: brightness, the edges between light and dark, and movement, no AI, nothing guessed — with tiny particles streaming along whatever it sees. A live camera can never be re-run exactly, so Capture freezes one moment of what it’s seeing into a fixed, signed snapshot you can draw and save. Field shows you that raw map of light, right next to the art — the honest truth of what it sees, never a black box.

This instrument needs JavaScript — it generates every line at runtime. The algorithms are plain, dependency-free code in system/atelier.js.

No outside code, no saved pictures — just a tiny random-number maker that starts from the seed, the drawing programs themselves, a canvas, and a tidier that stitches and orders the lines for a real pen. Why it belongs here: a drawing that always comes out the same from a stated seed, and carries that fingerprint of its own shape, is one you can re-run for yourself instead of taking on trust — the same standard I hold the rest of my work to, in a form you can hang on a wall.

Verify — re-derive it, don’t trust it.

Every saved file carries its seed and a SHA-256 witness of its own shape — SHA-256 is just a standard way to turn the exact drawing into a short, unique fingerprint, so the tiniest change shows up. Drop a file back here and two of the tools go to work on it. The witness rebuilds the drawing from the seed and works out the fingerprint again, using the browser’s own math — the very same move it makes on any file — and the gate, the checkpoint that says no until it’s sure, decides whether to accept it. If the fingerprints line up, that’s a MATCH and the gate lets it through. If even one point moved or the fingerprint was touched, that’s DRIFT and it turns it away. And if there’s no seed to check against, it’s UNVERIFIABLE — it won’t pretend, so it hands the decision to a person.

The witness and the gate here aren’t imitations made to look the part — they are the very same code those tools run, pulled out into one shared backbone that the tools all build on. One tool, EMET, takes the fingerprint of the drawing; another, proof-surface, is the checkpoint that rules on the result. Why it matters: the tools no longer just check themselves — they check each other’s work, and that mutual checking is the idea the whole project is built on.

Twenty-one algorithms, four photographs.

Each one is a real method for growing a pattern, with a long history in mathematics and in pen-plotter art — and each was chosen because one of the photographs already wears that exact shape in real life.

Phyllotaxisfrom the snail Vogel’s model: a seed placed every golden angle of turn, radius growing as the square root of its index. The same logarithmic packing a snail builds its shell by — and the Fibonacci spiral arms emerge on their own.
Flow fieldfrom the dandelion Thousands of particles traced through a field of angles drawn from layered noise, bent toward the light in the photograph — so the strokes radiate the way the ray-florets do, then break into turbulence.
Differential growthfrom the seedhead A closed curve that pushes away from itself and lengthens, inserting new points where it stretches — the same buckling that curls a drying seedhead into folds. You watch it convolve in real time.
Venationfrom the mallow Space colonisation: veins grow from a root toward scattered sources of auxin, thickening as their subtree grows — the algorithm botanists use to model exactly the leaf-veins the mallow wears.
Reaction–diffusionfrom the katydids Gray–Scott: two chemicals diffuse at different rates while one feeds on the other, and the field self-organises into Turing patterns — the mathematics behind spots, mazes, and a katydid’s speckle. Drawn as iso-contours; the photograph’s light sets which regime forms where.
Slime mouldfrom the seedhead Thousands of agents each lay a trail and steer toward where it — and the photograph’s light — runs strongest, reinforcing the busiest routes until a transport network sets. The way a Physarum slime mould reticulates between food, and solves a maze on the way. The lines are the foragers’ own paths; the network is emergent, never drawn.
Boidsfrom the flock Reynolds’ three rules — separation, alignment, cohesion — summed over the neighbours inside each agent’s vision, found through a grid hash. No leader and no drawn path: the murmuration is emergent, and the photograph’s light pulls the flock toward the bright tissue. After Reynolds, SIGGRAPH 1987.
Orbitalsfrom Schrödinger, 1926 The exact quantum solution for hydrogen: the wavefunction ψn,l = Rnl(r)·Yl0(θ), an associated-Laguerre radial part times a real spherical harmonic. We plot the probability density |ψ|² as iso-probability contour rings — the gaps are the orbital’s nodes, n−l−1 radial and l angular, the surfaces where the electron is never found. The default 3dz² is the atom’s own geometry. Visualization after Kavan (kevkev‑70).
Hilberta continuous fill Hilbert’s 1891 space-filling curve: one continuous stroke that passes through every cell of the plane exactly once, the specimen rendered as that single path. The geometry is pure integer arithmetic — no sines, no roots — so the drawing re-derives bit-for-bit on any machine. The strongest provenance the instrument can offer.
Pascalthe binomial theorem The binomial coefficients C(i,j) = C(i−1,j−1) + C(i−1,j) — Pascal’s triangle — taken mod m: the non-zero cells are a self-similar fractal, and mod 2 is the Sierpiński gasket. Pure integer arithmetic, so the drawing re-derives bit-for-bit on any machine; the photograph only tints the structure the coefficients already hold.
Harmonographa drawing machine Two damped pendulums per axis — the 19th-century harmonograph. Each axis sums a pair of sine swings whose amplitude decays, and near-integer frequency ratios make the figure slowly precess into a dense Lissajous weave. A real mechanical phenomenon, drawn as one continuous line; the specimen colours it as it passes.
Gosperthe flowsnake A space-filling fractal curve: one continuous stroke that tiles the plane in hexagons, a coastline that never crosses itself. Built by the Lindenmayer rewrite A→A-B--B+A++AA+B- at 60°; its six directions use only ½ and √3/2, so — like Hilbert — it re-derives bit-for-bit on any machine. The hexagonal sibling to the square Hilbert fill.
Dragona folded curve The Heighway dragon: fold a strip in half the same way n times, unfold every crease to a right angle, and a self-similar curve that tiles the plane without ever crossing itself appears. Pure 90° integer turns — no trigonometry — so it re-derives bit-for-bit on any machine.
Kochthe snowflake Von Koch’s 1904 construction: replace the middle third of every edge with an outward equilateral bump, forever — the first published curve of finite area but unbounded length. Its only constants are ½ and √3/2, so it too re-derives bit-for-bit on any machine.
DLAcoral & frost Diffusion-limited aggregation (Witten–Sander, 1981): random walkers drift until they touch the cluster and freeze where they land, so the tips shadow the interior and the form keeps branching — the geometry of coral, frost ferns, mineral dendrites, and electrodeposition. Held on an integer grid, so it re-derives bit-for-bit.
Voronoithe cell walls A Voronoi tessellation: every point of the plane joins its nearest seed, and the borders are the walls between territories — the partition a foam, a dragonfly’s wing, or a sheet of cells settles into. Each cell is found by exact half-plane clipping; Lloyd relaxation evens them. The specimen places the seeds.
L‑systemthe plant grammar A Lindenmayer system: a single rewrite rule applied to its own output, then walked by a turtle — the grammar Lindenmayer wrote in 1968 to model how a plant elaborates itself. Brackets push and pop the branch; the seed jitters every fork, so no two plants repeat.
Penrosea quasicrystal A Penrose tiling: Robinson triangles deflated in the golden ratio φ cover the plane with five-fold symmetry but never repeat — the aperiodic order long thought impossible, then found in real quasicrystals. The seed star uses sin/cos, so it re-derives deterministically rather than bit-for-bit.
Lightninga discharge Recursive midpoint displacement: a channel splits, kicks sideways, and forks at every halving — the branched path a Lichtenberg figure or a lightning stroke takes to ground through a dielectric. The geometry is perpendicular offset and arithmetic; only the fork angle calls a trig function.
Maurer rosea rhodonea A Maurer rose: walk the rose curve r = sin(nθ) in fixed integer-degree strides, and the straight chords between samples weave a lattice the smooth petals only imply — intricate order surfacing from a deliberately coarse sampling. After Peter Maurer, 1987.
Attractordeterministic chaos A Clifford attractor: iterate x′ = sin(ay) + c·cos(ax), y′ = sin(bx) + d·cos(by), and the orbit never repeats yet never escapes — a strange attractor, deterministic chaos folding a plane into a cloud. Every seed bends the constants c, d into a new creature.
